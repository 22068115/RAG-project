Overview
This level one subject provides a solid foundation in the theory and applications of differential calculus, as well as some introductory work on complex numbers. It is the first of two subjects developing aspects of calculus.Assumed Knowledge: Mathematics achieved at Bands 5-6, or knowledge equivalent to MATH1001 Analysis of Change.
This level one subject provides a solid foundation in the theory and applications of differential calculus, as well as some introductory work on complex numbers. It is the first of two subjects developing aspects of calculus.


Assumed Knowledge: Mathematics achieved at Bands 5-6, or knowledge equivalent to MATH1001 Analysis of Change.

Learning Outcomes
Collapse all
Upon completion of this subject, graduates will be able to:
1.
Define and operate with complex numbers.

keyboard_arrow_down
2.
Define and manipulate the following functions: exponential, trigonometric, hyperbolic, logarithmic, inverse trig and inverse hyperbolic.

keyboard_arrow_down
3.
Find limits of functions and determine if a function is continuous or differentiable.

keyboard_arrow_down
4.
Find the derivatives of functions.

keyboard_arrow_down
5.
Apply correctly techniques of differential calculus to problems involving optimization, curve sketching and rates of change.

keyboard_arrow_down
6.
Calculate basic integrals.

Level Restriction

keyboard_arrow_down
Students may complete the following subjects - MATH1026 Quantitative Thinking, MATH1001 Analysis of Change and MATH1014 Mathematics 1A in the following order:

MATH1026 Quantitative Thinking before MATH1001 Analysis of Change, but NOT after successfully completing MATH1001 Analysis of Change.

Students may take MATH1026 Quantitative Thinking or MATH1001 Analysis of Change BEFORE MATH1014 Mathematics 1A, but NOT after successfully completing MATH1014 Mathematics 1A.

Students cannot take a lower-level mathematics subject (i.e. MATH1026) after successfully completing a high-level one (MATH1001 or MATH1014) because the material in the lower-level subject is already assumed knowledge. Students may not register into any two of these subjects in the same teaching session.
